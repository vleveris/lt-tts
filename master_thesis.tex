\subsection*{Motivation}

Text-to-Speech (TTS) synthesis is a field of speech technology that focuses on converting written text into natural-sounding human speech. Over the past decades, TTS systems have evolved from simple rule-based mechanisms producing robotic and monotonous speech into highly advanced neural systems capable of generating expressive, fluent, and human-like audio. Today, TTS technology plays an important role in modern human–computer interaction and has become an integral component of many everyday applications, including virtual assistants (e.g. Siri or Alexa)~\cite{hoy2018alexa}, accessibility tools~\cite{teixeira2009speech, sharma2012speech}, navigation systems, audiobooks, customer service automation, and language learning or text translation platforms~\cite{kavitha2024enhancing}.

The main purpose of speech synthesis is to produce an artificial speech which sounds natural and expressive like a real human voice.

The growing demand for natural speech interfaces is driven by rapid advancements in artificial intelligence (AI), particularly in deep learning and neural network architectures. Neural TTS systems have significantly improved speech naturalness, prosody modeling, and pronunciation accuracy compared to earlier approaches like formant or concatenative synthesis. These systems learn the mapping from text directly to speech waveforms or intermediate acoustic representations, reducing the need for handcrafted rules and linguistic expertise. Modern TTS solutions are more scalable and adaptable across different languages and speaking styles. Also, it's much more easier to develop new AI-based TTS systems comparing to classical methods as they require much less linguistic knowledge and manual effort, and can be trained on large datasets to capture the nuances of natural speech.

One of the main challenges in TTS systems based on neural networks is the need for large amounts of high-quality, annotated speech data for training. For widely spoken languages, such datasets are often available, but for many smaller languages, collecting and annotating sufficient data can be difficult and expensive. Languages that lack large, publicly available speech corpora and tools for speech synthesis and natural language processing are often referred to as \emph{low-resource languages}. Despite the impressive progress achieved for popular languages such as English, Chinees or Spanish, many smaller or low-resource languages remain underrepresented in TTS research and development. Lithuanian, a Baltic language with complex morphology, rich inflection, and distinctive phonetic characteristics, is one such language. Developing high-quality neural TTS systems for Lithuanian presents both linguistic and technical challenges, making it an interesting and relevant research topic.

This Master’s thesis focuses on end-to-end neural text-to-speech synthesis for the Lithuanian language, with the goal of evaluating state-of-the-art TTS models, training them on Lithuanian speech data, and experimentally assessing their performance and suitability for this language.

\subsection*{Applications of Text-to-Speech Technology}

The importance of TTS technology extends beyond convenience and entertainment. One of its most impactful application areas is accessibility. For individuals with visual impairments, reading difficulties, or certain motor disabilities, TTS systems enable access to digital content, educational materials, and communication tools~\cite{chalamandaris2010unit, reddy2023speech}. Screen readers and voice-based interfaces rely heavily on high-quality speech synthesis to provide an inclusive user experience.

Another major application domain is human–computer interaction. Voice assistants such as smart speakers, mobile assistants, and in-car systems use TTS to communicate information naturally and efficiently~\cite{mohasi2006text}. As voice-based interaction becomes more prevalent, users increasingly expect synthesized speech to be expressive, emotionally appropriate, and indistinguishable from human speech.

TTS is also widely used in education and language learning, where it can provide pronunciation examples, reading assistance, and interactive learning experiences. In media and content creation, TTS enables the automated generation of audiobooks, news narration, podcasts, and video voiceovers, significantly reducing production costs and time.

In addition, TTS technology has applications in telecommunications, customer support automation, assistive robotics, and game development, where dynamic and context-aware speech synthesis enhances realism and user engagement. These diverse applications highlight the societal and economic value of advancing TTS research, particularly for languages that currently lack high-quality solutions.

\subsection*{Challenges for Lithuanian TTS}

Lithuanian language presents specific challenges for TTS systems due to its rich morphology, complex stress patterns, and phonetic distinctions. The language exhibits a large number of inflected forms, which increases vocabulary size and complicates text normalization and pronunciation modeling. Stress placement in Lithuanian can vary and plays an important role in meaning and naturalness of speech.

Lithuanian is considered a low-resource language in the context of speech technology, as publicly available, high-quality speech datasets are limited compared to major world languages. This makes it particularly interesting to study how modern neural TTS architectures perform under data constraints and what strategies can be used to improve synthesis quality.

Developing effective TTS systems for Lithuanian is not only academically relevant but also socially meaningful. High-quality Lithuanian TTS could support accessibility, education, digital preservation of the language, and the development of local AI-driven products and services.

\section{Overview of Text-to-Speech Synthesis}

This chapter reviews the development of TTS synthesis from rule-based and signal-processing methods to modern neural approaches. Section~\ref{sec:tts-overview} introduces TTS as a problem, defines its core components, and outlines the key challenges that persist regardless of the underlying technology. Sections~\ref{sec:classical} and~\ref{sec:neural} then trace the evolution of synthesis methods chronologically, covering classical approaches - formant, concatenative, and statistical parametric synthesis - followed by the neural architectures that have since become dominant.

\subsection{Text-to-Speech Synthesis as a Problem}
\label{sec:tts-overview}

Text-to-speech synthesis is the task of converting an arbitrary sequence of written characters into intelligible and natural-sounding speech. At a high level, a TTS system must solve two coupled sub-problems: determining \emph{what} to say (linguistic interpretation of the text) and \emph{how} to say it (acoustic realisation). The first sub-problem involves text normalisation, lexical disambiguation, grapheme-to-phoneme conversion, and prosody prediction; the second involves generating a waveform that encodes the target phonemes with appropriate pitch, energy, timing, and voice quality.

A fundamental difficulty is that the mapping from text to speech is \emph{one-to-many}: a single written sentence can be pronounced in infinitely many valid ways differing in pitch range, speaking rate, emphasis, and emotional colouring, none of which is encoded in the orthographic input~\cite{wang2017tacotron, ren2021fastspeech}. Human speakers resolve this ambiguity through pragmatic context, intention, and personality - information that is absent from raw text. TTS systems must therefore either predict a single realisation from a learned data, or explicitly model the space of possible prosodic realisations, a problem that remains an active area of research. 

Challenges arise from the diversity of natural language. Text normalization must handle numerals, abbreviations, acronyms, foreign words, and domain-specific terminology, all of which require language- and context-dependent expansion rules. Grapheme-to-phoneme conversion is non-trivial in languages with irregular spelling, while languages with lexical stress or tone must correctly predict these features to avoid changing word meaning. Sentence-level prosody - the placement of emphasis, phrasing boundaries, and intonational contours - depends on syntactic structure, information structure, and discourse context, and predicting it reliably from text alone remains an open problem.

Computational cost is another big concern. High-quality neural waveform generation is computationally intensive: a model that produces 24~kHz audio must effectively predict 24,000 samples per second, each dependent on prior context. Autoregressive models address this by generating samples sequentially, at the cost of latency; parallel models can synthesise in a single forward pass but require more complex training procedures. Striking the right balance between quality, latency, and hardware requirements is particularly important for deployment on edge devices or in real-time interactive applications~\cite{kong2020hifi}.

Additional problems arise for low-resource languages. Most advances in TTS have been driven by research on English and a small number of other well-resourced languages, for which hundreds of hours of studio-quality recordings and large pronunciation lexicons are freely available. For low-resource languages such as Lithuanian, publicly available speech corpora are scarce and often limited in speaker diversity, recording conditions, and stylistic variety. Morphologically rich languages present an additional difficulty: the large number of inflected word forms increases lexical sparsity, complicates text normalisation, and reduces unit coverage in corpus-based approaches. As a result, models trained on low-resource data tend to produce less natural speech and generalise poorly to out-of-vocabulary words~\cite{hasanabadi2023overview}.

\subsection{Classical Approaches to TTS Synthesis}
\label{sec:classical}

A complete TTS system consists of two main processing stages. The \emph{front end} (text analysis) converts raw input text into a linguistic representation by performing text normalization, part-of-speech tagging, grapheme-to-phoneme conversion, and prosody prediction. The \emph{back end} (speech synthesis) uses this representation to generate an audio waveform. Classical TTS systems differ primarily in how the back end operates.

\subsubsection{Formant Synthesis}

Formant synthesis is one of the earliest approaches to machine speech production. It models the human vocal tract as a set of electronic filters that mimic the natural resonances (called formants) of the vocal tract. A formant synthesizer generates speech by passing a source signal through these filters and varying their settings over time to produce different sounds.

The most influential system of this kind was the Klatt synthesizer~\cite{klatt1980software}, which used about 40 adjustable parameters to control pitch, volume, and filter characteristics. Commercial products like DECTalk were built on this design and widely used in assistive technology during the 1980s and 1990s~\cite{hallahan1995dectalk}.

Formant synthesis offers practical advantages: the synthesizer itself is compact, requires no recorded speech database, and allows precise control over every acoustic parameter. However, producing natural-sounding output requires extensive phonological and phonetic expertise, and the handcrafted rules must be re-developed from scratch for each new language. In practice, formant-based systems produce speech that sounds robotic and monotonous, and they have largely been superseded by data-driven approaches~\cite{reddy2023speech}.

\subsubsection{Concatenative Synthesis}

Concatenative synthesis builds speech by selecting and joining pre-recorded speech segments from a database, relying on the naturalness of real human recordings.

The simplest form uses \emph{diphones} - segments that span from the middle of one phoneme to the middle of the next, capturing the transition between them~\cite{dutoit1997high}. A complete diphone set for a language can be recorded in a short session with one speaker. After joining the segments, pitch and timing are adjusted using signal processing techniques such as TD-PSOLA~\cite{laprie1998automatic, toma2010td}.

A more advanced variant is \emph{unit selection} synthesis~\cite{hunt1996unit}, which uses a large speech corpus - typically several hours of recordings - and indexes all available speech segments. During synthesis, a search algorithm finds the best sequence of segments by balancing two factors: how well each segment matches the desired sound, and how smoothly consecutive segments connect at their boundaries. When good matches are found, the output can sound very natural.

Concatenative synthesis was the leading commercial TTS approach in the 1990s and 2000s~\cite{zhou2021unitnet, schwarz2006real, hunt1996unit}. It runs efficiently on basic hardware, making it suitable for embedded devices and real-time applications~\cite{chalamandaris2010unit}. However, it has important limitations: it requires large, expensive recording databases; it is tied to a single speaker's voice; expressive speech is difficult to produce; and for morphologically rich languages like Lithuanian, many phonetic contexts may be missing from the corpus, leading to noticeable drops in quality. The concatenation points can also introduce audible artifacts. The search algorithm can select segments which have different acoustic characteristics, pitch or energy if the database is small. Also, developing concatenative systems requires significant linguistic expertise and a lot of manual work to design the recording script and ensure good coverage of phonetic contexts.

\subsubsection{Statistical Parametric Speech Synthesis}

Statistical parametric speech synthesis (SPSS) addresses the size and coverage limitations of concatenative methods by replacing the recording database with a compact statistical model. The main approach uses Hidden Markov Models (HMM)~\cite{zen2009statistical, tokuda2013speech}.

Instead of storing speech segments, these TTS systems learn statistical patterns from pre-recorded audio. SPSS model averages acoustic features of similar-sounding speech across all instances of a given phonetic context in the training data. First of all, a large speech corpus is recorded and annotated with phonetic transcriptions. Then the system extracts acoustic features (such as spectral shape and pitch) from the recordings and trains the HMM model to predict these features for any given text input. Usually, a maximum likelihood criterion is used to estimate the model parameters, which results in a single average prediction for each phonetic context. Since many phonetic contexts are rare in the training data, the model groups similar contexts together using decision trees, allowing it to generalize to unseen combinations~\cite{zen2009statistical}. During synthesis, the model generates a sequence of acoustic features, which vocoder then converts into an audio waveform~\cite{tokuda2013speech}.

A key advantage of SPSS is its flexibility. The voice can be adapted to a new speaker using only a small amount of recordings, and speaking style or emotion can be controlled by blending different trained models~\cite{zen2009statistical, tokuda2013speech}. To achieve this, several methods have been proposed, such as maximum likelihood linear regression~\cite{leggetter1995maximum}, speaker adaptive training~\cite{anastasakos1997speaker} and interpolation (voice-mixing allows to generate speech for the speaker that the model was not trained on). The model itself is also very compact - just a few megabytes compared to the gigabytes needed for concatenative databases - and it can handle words and phonetic contexts not present in the training data, which is valuable for morphologically rich languages.

However, the statistical averaging used in training produces over-smoothed output, resulting in muffled speech that lacks fine detail. The vocoder reconstruction also adds a characteristic buzziness. These quality limitations were the main motivation for the shift to neural network approaches. Comparing to concatenative synthesis, SPSS is more flexible and requires less data, but it produces lower quality speech. The quality gap is particularly noticeable for languages with complex phonetic contexts, where the model's generalization can lead to unnatural-sounding output.

\subsection{Emergence of Neural TTS Systems}
\label{sec:neural}

The limitations of statistical parametric synthesis such as over-smoothed output and vocoder artifacts led to deep learning approaches that learn richer acoustic representations directly from data.
This transition happened in stages: first replacing individual SPSS components with neural networks, then developing fully neural vocoders, and finally building systems that map text to waveforms in a single model.
Progress in neural network research, combined with greater availability of computational power and resources, particularly the increasing popularity of GPUs for training, enabled the creation of several TTS neural network architectures that outperformed traditional methods.
The progression of neural TTS architectures can be understood as a series of deliberate trade-offs between quality, speed, simplicity, and controllability.
Rather than a single breakthrough, the field evolved incrementally - each generation addressing the hardest limitations of the previous one.

In this section we will present the main neural architectures and their evolution.

\subsubsection{Two-Stage TTS Models}

Traditional speech synthesis software relied on a hand-crafted front-end to extract linguistic features from text, paired with an explicit duration model and a signal-processing-based vocoder.
Neural networks initially entered this field by replacing individual components but still preserving the overall two-stage structure, and that structure remains popular today.

In such a pipeline, an \emph{acoustic model} takes text (or phoneme sequences) as input and produces intermediate acoustic features.
The most common representation is the mel-spectrogram: a time-frequency map of spectral energy computed on a perceptually-motivated frequency scale.
The acoustic model therefore performs a text-to-spectrogram conversion.
A separate \emph{vocoder} model then converts those acoustic features into a time-domain audio waveform, performing the inverse spectrogram-to-waveform step.
Mel-spectrograms are a natural intermediate representation because they are compact, closely related to how humans perceive sound, and easier to predict accurately than raw waveforms.

Some of the early neural TTS models can be called multi-stage models if several other intermediate layers are used, for example, a separate frontend model for text preprocessing, but the two main classical components are the acoustic model and the vocoder.

The two-component decomposition introduces trade-offs.
Because the acoustic model and the vocoder are trained independently, the vocoder is exposed to ground-truth mel-spectrograms during training, but at inference it receives predicted ones, meaning the acoustic model is optimized for spectrogram accuracy rather than final audio quality.
This train-test mismatch can introduce artifacts.
Furthermore, keeping two separate training pipelines increases overall training / inference time and complicates deployment.

Despite these limitations, the acoustic model and vocoder paradigm produced highly natural-sounding speech and drove rapid progress in TTS field.

\subsubsubsection{Early neural approaches}

The earliest neural TTS systems, such as those inspired by the DeepSpeech line of work~\cite{hannun2014deep}, replaced the rule-based components of traditional pipelines with neural sub-modules while keeping the overall multi-stage structure the same.
A neural front-end extracted linguistic features, a separate neural acoustic model predicted acoustic parameters, and a classical vocoder reconstructed the waveform.
Although this approach improved naturalness over HMM-based SPSS, it preserved the fundamental weaknesses of modular design:
each component required independent training, errors from earlier stages generated more errors in later ones, and the overall system was difficult to improve.

\subsubsubsection{Autoregressive Acoustic Models}

Even a single second of audio data contains more than 22000 samples.
A lot of computational resources are required for a neural network model to predict such a sequence sample by sample.
Models that are dependent on previously generated outputs in a sequence are called autoregressive models~\cite{akaike1971autoregressive}.
Autoregressive acoustic models generate the output spectrogram one frame at a time, where each predicted frame is conditioned on all previously generated frames together with the input text.
This sequential formulation matches the natural left-to-right structure of speech and makes it straightforward to model dependencies between neighbouring frames, which contributes to smooth transitions and natural-sounding prosody.
Most autoregressive systems are built around an encoder--decoder architecture coupled with an attention mechanism that learns a soft alignment between the input text and the output frames, removing the need for explicit duration modelling or external aligners.

The main strength of this family of models is the high perceptual quality they can achieve with relatively simple training objectives.
The downsides follow directly from the sequential decoding: synthesis time grows linearly with utterance length, so real-time inference is difficult, and the learned attention can drift or collapse on long or unusual inputs, leading to skipped words, repeated syllables, or premature termination.

\paragraph{Tacotron.}
A huge impact came with Tacotron~\cite{wang2017tacotron}, developed by Google research team, which replaced the hand-crafted front-end with a single encoder-attention-decoder network trained on character-audio pairs.
It is the most popular among autoregressive models, based on recurrent neural networks, or, more precisely, on gated recurrent units (GRU)~\cite{cho2014learning} and a modified sequence-to-sequence architecture~\cite{sutskever2014sequence}.
By learning the alignment between text and spectrogram frames through a soft attention mechanism, Tacotron eliminated the need for explicit phoneme dictionaries or duration models.
It can synthesize speech directly from text characters.
However, attention-based alignment is fragile: the model can lose track of its position and produce repeated or skipped words, especially on longer sequences.
Training and inference are slow, because the decoder predicts frames one at a time sequentially.
The original Tacotron relied on the Griffin--Lim vocoder algorithm to invert spectrograms to audio, which introduced a characteristic synthetic quality unrelated to the acoustic model's accuracy.
If specific texts were not used during training, the model can fail to generalize and produces unintelligible output.
Authors of Tacotron paper agreed that vocoder part is the thing to be improved for better quality of the output audio.

\paragraph{Tacotron~2.}
Tacotron~2~\cite{shen2018natural}, a successor to the original Tacotron, addressed the vocoder bottleneck by substituting Griffin--Lim with a modified WaveNet~\cite{oord2016wavenet} conditioned on mel-spectrograms rather than on linguistic features.
One of its standout enhancements was in its structural design.
The encoder in Tacotron~2 integrated a higher number of convolutional layers, allowing it to better capture text features, particularly beneficial for longer text.
The algorithm for generating mel-spectrograms using feature-prediction architecture was slightly changed as well.
This model introduced a post-processing network to refine the generated Mel spectrogram, which in turn elevated the overall speech quality.
It was presented the same year as the original Tacotron model - in 2018.
This modified version of Tacotron outperformed the best parametric and concatenative TTS systems of that time, reaching a mean opinion score (MOS) close to natural speech.
It's very popular and widely used even today because of its simplicity for training and good audio quality.
The major problem was inference speed: WaveNet generates one sample at a time, making real-time synthesis impractical without significant hardware acceleration.
The two-model pipeline also means the acoustic model is trained to minimise spectrogram prediction error, not perceptual audio quality - a mismatch that persists at inference.
Also, the attention-based alignment still remains fragile, and the model can fail on longer or unusual inputs.
NVIDIA developed its own implementation of Tacotron~2 by utilizing a WaveGlow instead of the original WaveNet, but the system was still relatively slow and especially struggled with longer sequences.

\paragraph{Transformer TTS.}
Before describing Transformer TTS, it is worth briefly explaining what a Transformer actually is.
The Transformer is a type of neural network architecture introduced in 2017~\cite{vaswani2017attention} that quickly became the dominant approach in natural language processing and, soon after, in many other areas of deep learning.
Unlike recurrent neural networks, which read a sequence one element at a time and pass information forward step by step, a Transformer looks at the entire input sequence at once.
Its key idea is a mechanism called \emph{self-attention}, which lets every position in the sequence directly compare itself with every other position and decide which ones are most relevant.
When the model is processing a particular word, character or phoneme, self-attention helps it categorize the particular symbol by comparing it to the other symbols using all input as context, and weight them accordingly.

This design has two important practical consequences.
First, because all positions are processed in parallel rather than one after another, Transformers train much faster than recurrent networks on modern hardware such as GPUs.
Second, the model can easily capture relationships between distant parts of a sequence, since any two positions are only one attention step apart.
These properties make Transformers especially attractive for tasks like TTS, where the pronunciation of a word can depend on context far away in the sentence.

Once Transformer architectures proved effective in natural language processing, they were applied to TTS.
Transformer TTS~\cite{li2019neural} replaced the recurrent layers in Tacotron with multi-head self-attention, enabling more parallelism during training and better modelling of long-range dependencies in text.
Authors use phoneme sequences as inputs to the model to solve pronunciation problems.
Training was faster and the model generalised somewhat better to unusual phrasing, but inference remained autoregressive and therefore slow.
Like Tacotron~2, it still relied on an external vocoder and was susceptible to attention collapse on long utterances.

Despite the architectural differences between recurrent and Transformer-based TTS systems, they share the same fundamental limitations that come from their autoregressive nature.
Mel-spectrogram frames are still produced one after another, so synthesis time grows linearly with the length of the utterance and real-time generation is hard to achieve, especially for longer sentences.
The learned attention is also fragile: on long or unusual inputs, the model can lose track of its position and produce skipped or repeated words, unintelligible passages, or premature silence.
Finally, these systems offer little explicit control over prosody, since duration, pitch and energy emerge implicitly from the attention mechanism rather than from dedicated predictors.

Subsequent work such as Parallel WaveNet~\cite{oord2018parallel}, ClariNet~\cite{ping2018clarinet} and WaveGlow~\cite{prenger2019waveglow} addressed part of the problems of transformer-based and autoregressive models by replacing the slow WaveNet vocoder with much faster non-autoregressive alternatives.
However, these are only improved external vocoders.
They still need to be conditioned on mel-spectrograms produced by an autoregressive acoustic model.
As long as the spectrogram itself is generated frame by frame, the overall pipeline cannot become truly fast or robust.
To remove this final bottleneck, researchers turned to non-autoregressive acoustic models that generate all mel-frames in parallel.

\subsubsubsection{Non-autoregressive Acoustic Models}

Non-autoregressive acoustic models take a fundamentally different approach to spectrogram generation.
Instead of producing mel-frames one after another, where each frame depends on the previously generated ones, they output all frames at the same time in a single forward pass.
To do this, the model must know in advance how many frames each input symbol (typically a phoneme or character) should expand into, which is handled by an explicit \emph{duration predictor}.
The encoded text representation is then stretched according to the predicted durations and passed to a parallel decoder that emits the full mel-spectrogram in one step.

This shift brings several practical advantages.
Inference becomes much faster, since synthesis time no longer grows linearly with utterance length and modern GPUs can process all frames in parallel.
The models are also more robust: because alignment between text and frames is decided by the duration predictor rather than by an attention mechanism unfolding over time, the typical autoregressive failure modes such as skipped or repeated words, premature silence or attention collapse largely disappear.
Finally, separating duration (and often pitch and energy) into dedicated predictors makes prosody explicitly controllable at inference time~\cite{kim2021conditional}.
The trade-off is that these models depend on accurate duration information during training, which is either obtained from a teacher autoregressive model or extracted from forced alignments of the training data.

\paragraph{FastSpeech.}
One of the first non-autoregressive TTS models is FastSpeech~\cite{ren2019fastspeech}, developed by Microsoft.
To solve the speed and robustness problems of autoregressive decoders, it introduced a feed-forward Transformer with a duration predictor and length regulator, enabling decoding and frame generation parallel and making synthesis roughly 38 times faster than Tacotron~2 with similar quality.
A duration predictor, trained on durations from a pre-trained Tacotron model, decides how many frames each phoneme expands to.
The expanded phoneme representations are then processed by a parallel decoder.
Because duration is predicted explicitly rather than learned through attention, FastSpeech is already more robust - it does not skip or repeat words.
This model was a big improvement in terms of speed, robustness and determinism.
It inspired a whole family of non-autoregressive TTS models that followed.

The drawback is that the duration predictor must be trained on alignments extracted from a teacher autoregressive model, creating an indirect dependency on Tacotron-style systems.
Also, since it is an acoustic model, it still relies on a separately trained external vocoder to produce audio.
Training of duration predictor is further complicated by its need on a pre-existing model for aligning the data.
Certain critiques raise the possibility of a trade-off in terms of expressiveness when comparing to attention-based alternatives~\cite{ren2020fastspeech}.

\paragraph{FastSpeech~2.}
FastSpeech~2~\cite{ren2020fastspeech} was developed to address issues left unanswered by its predecessor.
Due to its non-autoregressive and deterministic nature, FastSpeech had limited prosody and expressiveness.
Although the speech was synthesized faster, voice quality lacked naturalness.
In FastSpeech~2, authors added a modified, stronger duration predictor and two additional predictors for pitch and energy, which were trained directly on ground-truth phoneme annotations rather than relying on a teacher model.
These features improved speech temporal properties and fine-grained prosodic controllability, naturalness and dynamics as an additional benefit.
Explicit prosodic controls also allow adjusting speaking rate, pitch, and energy independently at inference time.

However, FastSpeech~2 requires a third-party tool to generate phoneme alignments for training the duration predictor, which is problematic for low resource languages like Lithuanian, because there are not many tools to convert text characters to phonemes.
It still relies on a separately trained vocoder to produce audio.
The model is also more complex than FastSpeech, with multiple predictors and loss functions that require careful tuning.

\paragraph{FastPitch.}
FastPitch~\cite{lancucki2021fastpitch}, developed by NVIDIA, is a non-autoregressive acoustic model that builds on the FastSpeech architecture but conditions the feed-forward Transformer explicitly on the fundamental frequency (pitch) of each input symbol.
Its approach deviates from other text-to-speech models by prioritizing autonomy in the process of aligning audio with transcriptions.
A dedicated pitch predictor estimates a single average pitch value per phoneme during training, and these values are added to the encoded representation before length regulation, so the decoder receives both linguistic and prosodic information when generating the mel-spectrogram.
This state implies that there is no longer a dependence on external alignment models, such as Transformer TTS or Tacotron~2.
The model possesses an ability to forecast pitch contour, a characteristic that enhances the possibility of natural voice modulation in the resulting audio~\cite{liu2023comparative}.
Compared to FastSpeech, this simple conditioning leads to more natural-sounding prosody and noticeably reduces the muffled quality typical of early non-autoregressive models.
At inference, the predicted pitch contour can be manipulated directly to change intonation or emphasis without retraining, making the model attractive for expressive synthesis.
One of the notable advantages of FastPitch is that it's effitient in reducing audio artifacts, hence guaranteeing improved sound quality and accelerated training convergence.
Like FastSpeech, training requires phoneme durations from an external aligner or a teacher autoregressive model, and a separately trained vocoder (commonly HiFi-GAN) is still needed to produce the final waveform.
FastPitch and FastSpeech~2 were trained at a similar time, and they represent two different approaches to improving the quality of non-autoregressive TTS models: FastSpeech~2 focuses on adding explicit prosodic predictors, while FastPitch emphasizes conditioning on pitch to enhance naturalness.
An additional advantage of this system is its capability to make training easier as a multi-speaker model enhancing its adaptability.
FastPitch offers users a comprehensive range of options for manipulating synthesized speech, including control over prosody, speech speed, and energy levels.
Also, it has the ability to accept either graphemes or phonemes as input, providing easier training setup and wider potential applications for various languages.

\paragraph{Glow-TTS.}
Glow-TTS~\cite{kim2020glow} learns a monotonic alignment between phonemes and mel-spectrogram frames using a dynamic programming search algorithm.
Training works without any external phoneme-audio aligner and is based on normalizing flows.
The Glow-TTS encoder maps each character to a Gaussian Distribution, while the decoder uses WaveGlow model layers to convert each spectrogram frame into a latent vector.
The flow-based decoder allows exact likelihood training and fast, high-quality synthesis, especially for multi-speaker variant of model.
Because the alignment is learned internally and respects the left-to-right order of speech, the same way as humans read the text, the model is inherently robust to word skipping and longer sentences or phrases.
Its main components include an encoder similar to the one used in Transformer TTS~\cite{li2019neural}, a flow-based decoder that generates mel-spectrograms from latent variables (based on WaveGlow)~\cite{prenger2019waveglow}, and a duration predictor which follows FastSpeech approach~\cite{ren2019fastspeech}.
Monotonic alignment search is ignored during inference, so the model only uses the duration predictor.

Glow-TTS's lack of external aligners is a major advantage.
This model demonstrated that self-learned alignment could match the quality of attention-based models when generating speech 15 times faster than Tacotron~2 while offering better robustness and removing the need for a separately trained teacher model.
However, like most acoustic models, it still requires a separately trained vocoder to produce audio.

\subsubsection{End-to-end Models}

All two-stage systems share a structural weakness.
Training the acoustic model and vocoder separately creates a mismatch: the vocoder sees natural mel-spectrograms during training but imperfect predicted ones at inference.
Neither model is optimised for perceived audio quality directly.
End-to-end models avoid this by jointly training the full pipeline from text to waveform.

\paragraph{Char2Wav.}
One of the first attempts at an end-to-end TTS model was Char2Wav~\cite{sotelo2017char2wav}, developed at MILA.
It combined an attention-based bidirectional RNN reader that maps characters to vocoder features with a SampleRNN-based neural vocoder, and trained both components jointly so that the system could be optimized as a single network.
The work demonstrated that text-to-waveform synthesis without hand-crafted intermediate features is feasible, but the resulting speech quality was below that of contemporary two-stage systems and the autoregressive vocoder made inference slow.

\paragraph{FastSpeech~2s.}
The authors of FastSpeech~2 also presented FastSpeech~2s in the same article, an end-to-end variant in which the acoustic model and the vocoder are trained together rather than as separate modules.
The model relies on adversarial training, a waveform decoder inspired by Parallel WaveGAN~\cite{yamamoto2020parallel}, and combined waveform losses such as the multi-resolution STFT loss and the LSGAN discriminator loss.
As the main challenge the authors identified the large information gap between the text input (phonemes) and the raw waveform output, which makes the mapping difficult to learn directly.
Training is also slow and resource-intensive because the waveform decoder must be optimized end-to-end together with the rest of the model.

\paragraph{EATS.}
EATS (End-to-End Adversarial Text-to-Speech)~\cite{donahue2020end} maps raw character or phoneme sequences directly to waveforms using a fully differentiable, GAN-based pipeline.
The system is split into an aligner that predicts per-token durations and produces a low-frequency feature representation, and a convolutional decoder that upsamples those features into a waveform.
Training combines adversarial losses with auxiliary spectrogram-based reconstruction losses to stabilise learning.
EATS demonstrated that high-quality speech can be generated in a single feed-forward pass from text, although the quality at the time of publication still lagged slightly behind the best two-stage systems.

\paragraph{Wave Tacotron.}
Wave Tacotron~\cite{weiss2021wave} extends the original Tacotron architecture to predict the waveform directly instead of a mel-spectrogram, removing the need for a separate vocoder.
It keeps the encoder--attention--decoder structure, but the decoder generates short blocks of raw audio samples through a normalizing flow conditioned on the decoder state.
Because the model is trained end-to-end with a likelihood objective on the waveform itself, it avoids the train--test mismatch that affects two-stage pipelines.
Its main limitation is inherited from Tacotron: synthesis is autoregressive and therefore slow, and quality is still slightly behind that of strong two-stage pipelines.

\paragraph{VITS.}
VITS (Conditional Variational Autoencoder with Adversarial Learning for End-to-End Text-to-Speech)~\cite{kim2021conditional} is an advance TTS model which uses inovative methods to generate high-quality speech.
It combines a variational acoustic autoencoder model, a HiFi-GAN decoder and normalizing flow network and training them jointly using adversarial losses, so the decoder is exposed to imperfect latent representations from the very start of training.
Normalizing flows allow the model to learn a complex distribution of latent variables that can be sampled from at inference time.
The monotonic alignment search from Glow-TTS is incorporated to avoid external aligners.
The result is a single model that maps text directly to a waveform.
The posterior encoder maps mel-spectrograms to latent representations, a flow-based prior network learns the distribution of latent variables given text, and a HiFi-GAN decoder generates waveforms from sampled latents.
VITS paper authors used phoneme sequences as inputs to the model, but since the phoneme-audio alignment is learned internally and does not require external tools, any text representation like plain or accented graphemes can be used.

Additionally, VITS utilizes Adversarial Training.
This approach involves a generator and a discriminator.
The generator tries to produce audio that's as close to real data as possible, while the discriminator learns to differentiate between original and synthesized speech.
As training continues, the generator improves to fool the discriminator, thereby producing higher-quality audio.
In VITS, this strategy is applied directly on the audio waveform.
The adversarial and reconstruction losses are trained together, so the decoder learns from its own outputs from the start and the prior network is pushed to match the true posterior.
At inference, the prior network samples a latent variable from the text alone and the decoder produces the waveform directly, with no intermediate spectrogram.
VITS also emphasizes the importance of different pronunciations of the same phrases.
To capture different pitch, duration and rhythm, a stochastic duration predictor is used.

VITS closes the train--test mismatch.
It scored higher than Tacotron~2 with WaveGlow in subjective listening tests without sacrificing inference speed by generating speech in a single forward pass.
VITS not only produces high-quality speech but also offers explicit control over prosody through its latent variables, which can be manipulated at inference time to change speaking style, emotion or emphasis without retraining.
It is the most widely used single-stage model.
