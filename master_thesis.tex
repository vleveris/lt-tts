\subsection*{Motivation}

Text-to-Speech (TTS) synthesis is a field of speech technology that focuses on converting written text into natural-sounding human speech. Over the past decades, TTS systems have evolved from simple rule-based mechanisms producing robotic and monotonous speech into highly advanced neural systems capable of generating expressive, fluent, and human-like audio. Today, TTS technology plays an important role in modern human–computer interaction and has become an integral component of many everyday applications, including virtual assistants (e.g. Siri or Alexa)~\cite{hoy2018alexa}, accessibility tools~\cite{teixeira2009speech, sharma2012speech}, navigation systems, audiobooks, customer service automation, and language learning or text translation platforms~\cite{kavitha2024enhancing}.

The main purpose of speech synthesis is to produce an artificial speech which sounds natural and expressive like a real human voice.

The growing demand for natural speech interfaces is driven by rapid advancements in artificial intelligence (AI), particularly in deep learning and neural network architectures. Neural TTS systems have significantly improved speech naturalness, prosody modeling, and pronunciation accuracy compared to earlier approaches like formant or concatenative synthesis. These systems learn the mapping from text directly to speech waveforms or intermediate acoustic representations, reducing the need for handcrafted rules and linguistic expertise. Modern TTS solutions are more scalable and adaptable across different languages and speaking styles. Also, it's much more easier to develop new AI-based TTS systems comparing to classical methods as they require much less linguistic knowledge and manual effort, and can be trained on large datasets to capture the nuances of natural speech.

One of the main challenges in TTS systems based on neural networks is the need for large amounts of high-quality, annotated speech data for training. For widely spoken languages, such datasets are often available, but for many smaller languages, collecting and annotating sufficient data can be difficult and expensive. Languages that lack large, publicly available speech corpora and tools for speech synthesis and natural language processing are often referred to as \emph{low-resource languages}. Despite the impressive progress achieved for popular languages such as English, Chinees or Spanish, many smaller or low-resource languages remain underrepresented in TTS research and development. Lithuanian, a Baltic language with complex morphology, rich inflection, and distinctive phonetic characteristics, is one such language. Developing high-quality neural TTS systems for Lithuanian presents both linguistic and technical challenges, making it an interesting and relevant research topic.

This Master’s thesis focuses on end-to-end neural text-to-speech synthesis for the Lithuanian language, with the goal of evaluating state-of-the-art TTS models, training them on Lithuanian speech data, and experimentally assessing their performance and suitability for this language.

\subsection*{Applications of Text-to-Speech Technology}

The importance of TTS technology extends beyond convenience and entertainment. One of its most impactful application areas is accessibility. For individuals with visual impairments, reading difficulties, or certain motor disabilities, TTS systems enable access to digital content, educational materials, and communication tools~\cite{chalamandaris2010unit, reddy2023speech}. Screen readers and voice-based interfaces rely heavily on high-quality speech synthesis to provide an inclusive user experience.

Another major application domain is human–computer interaction. Voice assistants such as smart speakers, mobile assistants, and in-car systems use TTS to communicate information naturally and efficiently~\cite{mohasi2006text}. As voice-based interaction becomes more prevalent, users increasingly expect synthesized speech to be expressive, emotionally appropriate, and indistinguishable from human speech.

TTS is also widely used in education and language learning, where it can provide pronunciation examples, reading assistance, and interactive learning experiences. In media and content creation, TTS enables the automated generation of audiobooks, news narration, podcasts, and video voiceovers, significantly reducing production costs and time.

In addition, TTS technology has applications in telecommunications, customer support automation, assistive robotics, and game development, where dynamic and context-aware speech synthesis enhances realism and user engagement. These diverse applications highlight the societal and economic value of advancing TTS research, particularly for languages that currently lack high-quality solutions.

\subsection*{Challenges for Lithuanian TTS}

Lithuanian language presents specific challenges for TTS systems due to its rich morphology, complex stress patterns, and phonetic distinctions. The language exhibits a large number of inflected forms, which increases vocabulary size and complicates text normalization and pronunciation modeling. Stress placement in Lithuanian can vary and plays an important role in meaning and naturalness of speech.

Lithuanian is considered a low-resource language in the context of speech technology, as publicly available, high-quality speech datasets are limited compared to major world languages. This makes it particularly interesting to study how modern neural TTS architectures perform under data constraints and what strategies can be used to improve synthesis quality.

Developing effective TTS systems for Lithuanian is not only academically relevant but also socially meaningful. High-quality Lithuanian TTS could support accessibility, education, digital preservation of the language, and the development of local AI-driven products and services.

\section{Overview of Text-to-Speech Synthesis}

This chapter reviews the development of TTS synthesis from rule-based and signal-processing methods to modern neural approaches. Section~\ref{sec:tts-overview} introduces TTS as a problem, defines its core components, and outlines the key challenges that persist regardless of the underlying technology. Sections~\ref{sec:classical} and~\ref{sec:neural} then trace the evolution of synthesis methods chronologically, covering classical approaches - formant, concatenative, and statistical parametric synthesis - followed by the neural architectures that have since become dominant.

\subsection{Text-to-Speech Synthesis as a Problem}
\label{sec:tts-overview}

Text-to-speech synthesis is the task of converting an arbitrary sequence of written characters into intelligible and natural-sounding speech. At a high level, a TTS system must solve two coupled sub-problems: determining \emph{what} to say (linguistic interpretation of the text) and \emph{how} to say it (acoustic realisation). The first sub-problem involves text normalisation, lexical disambiguation, grapheme-to-phoneme conversion, and prosody prediction; the second involves generating a waveform that encodes the target phonemes with appropriate pitch, energy, timing, and voice quality.

A fundamental difficulty is that the mapping from text to speech is \emph{one-to-many}: a single written sentence can be pronounced in infinitely many valid ways differing in pitch range, speaking rate, emphasis, and emotional colouring, none of which is encoded in the orthographic input~\cite{wang2017tacotron, ren2021fastspeech}. Human speakers resolve this ambiguity through pragmatic context, intention, and personality - information that is absent from raw text. TTS systems must therefore either predict a single realisation from a learned data, or explicitly model the space of possible prosodic realisations, a problem that remains an active area of research. 

Challenges arise from the diversity of natural language. Text normalization must handle numerals, abbreviations, acronyms, foreign words, and domain-specific terminology, all of which require language- and context-dependent expansion rules. Grapheme-to-phoneme conversion is non-trivial in languages with irregular spelling, while languages with lexical stress or tone must correctly predict these features to avoid changing word meaning. Sentence-level prosody - the placement of emphasis, phrasing boundaries, and intonational contours - depends on syntactic structure, information structure, and discourse context, and predicting it reliably from text alone remains an open problem.

Computational cost is another big concern. High-quality neural waveform generation is computationally intensive: a model that produces 24~kHz audio must effectively predict 24,000 samples per second, each dependent on prior context. Autoregressive models address this by generating samples sequentially, at the cost of latency; parallel models can synthesise in a single forward pass but require more complex training procedures. Striking the right balance between quality, latency, and hardware requirements is particularly important for deployment on edge devices or in real-time interactive applications~\cite{kong2020hifi}.

Additional problems arise for low-resource languages. Most advances in TTS have been driven by research on English and a small number of other well-resourced languages, for which hundreds of hours of studio-quality recordings and large pronunciation lexicons are freely available. For low-resource languages such as Lithuanian, publicly available speech corpora are scarce and often limited in speaker diversity, recording conditions, and stylistic variety. Morphologically rich languages present an additional difficulty: the large number of inflected word forms increases lexical sparsity, complicates text normalisation, and reduces unit coverage in corpus-based approaches. As a result, models trained on low-resource data tend to produce less natural speech and generalise poorly to out-of-vocabulary words~\cite{hasanabadi2023overview}.

\subsection{Classical Approaches to TTS Synthesis}
\label{sec:classical}

A complete TTS system consists of two main processing stages. The \emph{front end} (text analysis) converts raw input text into a linguistic representation by performing text normalization, part-of-speech tagging, grapheme-to-phoneme conversion, and prosody prediction. The \emph{back end} (speech synthesis) uses this representation to generate an audio waveform. Classical TTS systems differ primarily in how the back end operates.

\subsubsection{Formant Synthesis}

Formant synthesis is one of the earliest approaches to machine speech production. It models the human vocal tract as a set of electronic filters that mimic the natural resonances (called formants) of the vocal tract. A formant synthesizer generates speech by passing a source signal through these filters and varying their settings over time to produce different sounds.

The most influential system of this kind was the Klatt synthesizer~\cite{klatt1980software}, which used about 40 adjustable parameters to control pitch, volume, and filter characteristics. Commercial products like DECTalk were built on this design and widely used in assistive technology during the 1980s and 1990s~\cite{hallahan1995dectalk}.

Formant synthesis offers practical advantages: the synthesizer itself is compact, requires no recorded speech database, and allows precise control over every acoustic parameter. However, producing natural-sounding output requires extensive phonological and phonetic expertise, and the handcrafted rules must be re-developed from scratch for each new language. In practice, formant-based systems produce speech that sounds robotic and monotonous, and they have largely been superseded by data-driven approaches~\cite{reddy2023speech}.

\subsubsection{Concatenative Synthesis}

Concatenative synthesis builds speech by selecting and joining pre-recorded speech segments from a database, relying on the naturalness of real human recordings.

The simplest form uses \emph{diphones} - segments that span from the middle of one phoneme to the middle of the next, capturing the transition between them~\cite{dutoit1997high}. A complete diphone set for a language can be recorded in a short session with one speaker. After joining the segments, pitch and timing are adjusted using signal processing techniques such as TD-PSOLA~\cite{laprie1998automatic, toma2010td}.

A more advanced variant is \emph{unit selection} synthesis~\cite{hunt1996unit}, which uses a large speech corpus - typically several hours of recordings - and indexes all available speech segments. During synthesis, a search algorithm finds the best sequence of segments by balancing two factors: how well each segment matches the desired sound, and how smoothly consecutive segments connect at their boundaries. When good matches are found, the output can sound very natural.

Concatenative synthesis was the leading commercial TTS approach in the 1990s and 2000s~\cite{zhou2021unitnet, schwarz2006real, hunt1996unit}. It runs efficiently on basic hardware, making it suitable for embedded devices and real-time applications~\cite{chalamandaris2010unit}. However, it has important limitations: it requires large, expensive recording databases; it is tied to a single speaker's voice; expressive speech is difficult to produce; and for morphologically rich languages like Lithuanian, many phonetic contexts may be missing from the corpus, leading to noticeable drops in quality. The concatenation points can also introduce audible artifacts. The search algorithm can select segments which have different acoustic characteristics, pitch or energy if the database is small. Also, developing concatenative systems requires significant linguistic expertise and a lot of manual work to design the recording script and ensure good coverage of phonetic contexts.

\subsubsection{Statistical Parametric Speech Synthesis}

Statistical parametric speech synthesis (SPSS) addresses the size and coverage limitations of concatenative methods by replacing the recording database with a compact statistical model. The main approach uses Hidden Markov Models (HMM)~\cite{zen2009statistical, tokuda2013speech}.

Instead of storing speech segments, these TTS systems learn statistical patterns from pre-recorded audio. SPSS model averages acoustic features of similar-sounding speech across all instances of a given phonetic context in the training data. First of all, a large speech corpus is recorded and annotated with phonetic transcriptions. Then the system extracts acoustic features (such as spectral shape and pitch) from the recordings and trains the HMM model to predict these features for any given text input. Usually, a maximum likelihood criterion is used to estimate the model parameters, which results in a single average prediction for each phonetic context. Since many phonetic contexts are rare in the training data, the model groups similar contexts together using decision trees, allowing it to generalize to unseen combinations~\cite{zen2009statistical}. During synthesis, the model generates a sequence of acoustic features, which vocoder then converts into an audio waveform~\cite{tokuda2013speech}.

A key advantage of SPSS is its flexibility. The voice can be adapted to a new speaker using only a small amount of recordings, and speaking style or emotion can be controlled by blending different trained models~\cite{zen2009statistical, tokuda2013speech}. To achieve this, several methods have been proposed, such as maximum likelihood linear regression~\cite{leggetter1995maximum}, speaker adaptive training~\cite{anastasakos1997speaker} and interpolation (voice-mixing allows to generate speech for the speaker that the model was not trained on). The model itself is also very compact - just a few megabytes compared to the gigabytes needed for concatenative databases - and it can handle words and phonetic contexts not present in the training data, which is valuable for morphologically rich languages.

However, the statistical averaging used in training produces over-smoothed output, resulting in muffled speech that lacks fine detail. The vocoder reconstruction also adds a characteristic buzziness. These quality limitations were the main motivation for the shift to neural network approaches. Comparing to concatenative synthesis, SPSS is more flexible and requires less data, but it produces lower quality speech. The quality gap is particularly noticeable for languages with complex phonetic contexts, where the model's generalization can lead to unnatural-sounding output.

\subsection{Emergence of Neural TTS Systems}
\label{sec:neural}

The limitations of statistical parametric synthesis such as over-smoothed output and vocoder artifacts led to deep learning approaches that learn richer acoustic representations directly from data. This transition happened in stages: first replacing individual SPSS components with neural networks, then developing fully neural vocoders, and finally building systems that map text to waveforms in a single model. Progress in neural network research, combined with greater availability of computational power and resources, particularly the increasing popularity of GPUs for training, enabled the creation of several TTS neural network architectures that outperformed traditional methods. In this section we will present the main neural architectures and their evolution. But before that it's necessary to introduce some basic concepts of neural networks relevant to TTS generation.

\subsubsection{Two-Stage TTS Models}

Traditional speech synthesis software relied on a hand-crafted front-end to extract linguistic features from text, paired with an explicit duration model and a signal-processing-based vocoder. Neural networks initially entered this field by replacing individual components but still preserving the overall two-stage structure, and that structure remains popular today.

In such a pipeline, an \emph{acoustic model} takes text (or phoneme sequences) as input and produces intermediate acoustic features. The most common representation is the mel-spectrogram: a time-frequency map of spectral energy computed on a perceptually-motivated frequency scale. The acoustic model therefore performs a text-to-spectrogram conversion. A separate \emph{vocoder} then converts those acoustic features into a time-domain audio waveform, performing the inverse spectrogram-to-waveform step.

Mel-spectrograms are a natural intermediate representation because they are compact, closely related to how humans perceive sound, and easier to predict accurately than raw waveforms. However, the two-component decomposition also introduces trade-offs. Because the acoustic model and the vocoder are trained independently, the vocoder is exposed to ground-truth mel-spectrograms during training, but at inference it receives predicted ones. This \emph{train--test mismatch} can introduce artifacts. Furthermore, keeping two separate training pipelines increases overall training / inference time and complicates deployment.

Despite these limitations, the acoustic model and vocoder paradigm produced highly natural-sounding speech and drove rapid progress in TTS field.

\subsubsection{Main TTS Models, Architectures and Their Evolution}

The progression of neural TTS architectures can be understood as a series of deliberate trade-offs between quality, speed, simplicity, and controllability. Rather than a single breakthrough, the field evolved incrementally - each generation addressing the hardest pressing limitations of the previous one.

\subsubsubsection{Early neural approaches}

The earliest neural TTS systems, such as those inspired by the DeepSpeech line of work~\cite{bahdanau2016end}, replaced the rule-based components of traditional pipelines with neural sub-modules while keeping the overall multi-stage structure the same. A neural front-end extracted linguistic features, a separate neural acoustic model predicted acoustic parameters, and a classical vocoder reconstructed the waveform. Although this approach improved naturalness over HMM-based SPSS, it preserved the fundamental weaknesses of modular design: each component required independent training, errors from earlier stages generated more errors in later ones, and the overall system was difficult to improve.

\subsubsubsection{Autoregressive Acoustic Models}

\subsubsubsubsection{Tacotron: sequence-to-sequence acoustic modelling}

A huge impact came with Tacotron~\cite{wang2017tacotron}, developed by Google research team, which replaced the hand-crafted front-end with a single encoder--attention--decoder network trained on character-audio pairs. It is based on recurrent neural networks, or, more precisely, on gated recurrent units (GRU)~\cite{cho2014learning} and a modified sequence-to-sequence architecture~\cite{sutskever2014sequence}. By learning the alignment between text and spectrogram frames through a soft attention mechanism, Tacotron eliminated the need for explicit phoneme dictionaries or duration models. It can synthesize speech directly from text characters. However, attention-based alignment is fragile: the model can lose track of its position and produce repeated or skipped words, especially on longer sequences. Training and inference is slow, because The decoder predicts frames one at a time, sequentially. The original Tacotron relied on the Griffin--Lim vocoder algorithm to invert spectrograms to audio, which introduced a characteristic synthetic quality unrelated to the acoustic model's accuracy. If specific texts were not used during training, the model can fail to generalize and produces unintelligible output. Authors of Tacotron paper agreed that vocoder part is the thing to be improved for better quality of the output audio.

Tacotron~2~\cite{shen2018natural}, a successor to the original Tacotron, addressed the vocoder bottleneck by substituting Griffin--Lim with a modified WaveNet~\cite{oord2016wavenet} conditioned on mel-spectrograms rather than on linguistic features. One of its standout enhancements was in its structural design. The encoder in Tacotron~2 integrated a higher number of convolutional layers, allowing it to better capture text features, particularly beneficial for longer text. The algorithm for generating mel-spectrograms using feature-prediction architecture was slightly changed as well. This model introduced a post-processing network to refine the generated Mel spectrogram, which in turn elevated the overal speech quality. It was presented the same year as the original Tacotron model - in 2018. This modified version of Tacotron outperformed the best parametric and concatenative TTS systems of that time, reaching a mean opinion score (MOS) close to natural speech. It's very popular and widely used even today because of its sumplicity for training and good audio quality. The major problem was inference speed: WaveNet generates one sample at a time, making real-time synthesis impractical without significant hardware acceleration. The two-model pipeline also means the acoustic model is trained to minimise spectrogram prediction error, not perceptual audio quality - a mismatch that persists at inference. Also, the attention-based alignment still remains fragile, and the model can fail on longer or unusual inputs.
NVIDIA developed its own implementation of Tacotron~2 by utilizing a WaveGlow instead of the original WaveNet, but the system was still relatively slow and especially struggled with longer sequences.

\paragraph{Transformer-based models.}
Once Transformer architectures~\cite{vaswani2017attention} proved effective in natural language processing, they were applied to TTS. Transformer TTS~\cite{li2019neural} replaced the recurrent layers in Tacotron with multi-head self-attention, enabling more parallelism during training and better modelling of long-range dependencies in text. Training was faster and the model generalised somewhat better to unusual phrasing, but inference remained autoregressive and therefore slow. Like Tacotron~2, it still relied on an external vocoder and was susceptible to attention collapse on long utterances.

\paragraph{Non-autoregressive acoustic models.}
The inference bottleneck of autoregressive decoders motivated a new class of models that generate all spectrogram frames simultaneously. FastSpeech~\cite{ren2019fastspeech} introduced a feed-forward Transformer with a duration predictor, enabling parallel decoding and making synthesis roughly 38 times faster than Tacotron~2. Because duration is predicted explicitly rather than learned through attention, FastSpeech is also far more robust: it does not skip or repeat words. The drawback is that the duration predictor must be trained on alignments extracted from a teacher autoregressive model, creating an indirect dependency on Tacotron-style systems. FastSpeech~2~\cite{ren2021fastspeech} removed this dependency by training duration, pitch, and energy predictors directly from ground-truth annotations, adding fine-grained prosodic controllability as an additional benefit.

\paragraph{Flow-based models and internal alignment.}
A different family of models addresses the alignment problem through normalizing flows. Glow-TTS~\cite{kim2020glow} learns a monotonic alignment between phonemes and mel-spectrogram frames using a dynamic programming search, without any external aligner. The flow-based decoder allows exact likelihood training and fast, high-quality synthesis. Critically, because the alignment is learned internally and respects the left-to-right order of speech, the model is inherently robust to word skipping. Glow-TTS demonstrated that self-learned alignment could match the quality of attention-based models while offering much better robustness. Like most acoustic models of its era, it still requires a separately trained vocoder to produce audio.

\paragraph{End-to-end models.}
All two-stage systems share a structural weakness: the vocoder is trained on ground-truth spectrograms but receives predicted ones at inference, and neither model is optimised for perceived audio quality directly. VITS~\cite{kim2021conditional} overcomes this by jointly training a variational acoustic model and a HiFi-GAN decoder using adversarial losses, so the decoder is exposed to imperfect latent representations from the very start of training. The monotonic alignment search from Glow-TTS is incorporated to avoid external aligners. The result is a single model that maps text directly to a waveform, closes the train--test mismatch, and surpasses two-stage baselines in listening tests without sacrificing inference speed.

\subsubsection{Neural Vocoders}

Alongside neural acoustic models, a separate line of research focused on waveform reconstruction. Traditional vocoders (STRAIGHT, WORLD) add a characteristic buzziness due to their source--filter approach; neural vocoders instead learn to generate waveforms directly from acoustic features.

\paragraph{Autoregressive vocoders.}
WaveNet~\cite{oord2016wavenet}, by van den Oord et al., was the first neural vocoder to match real recordings in listening tests. It generates each audio sample based on all previous samples and an acoustic conditioning signal, using stacks of dilated causal convolutions to cover a large time span without excessive depth. Despite its quality, WaveNet is slow: producing one second of 16~kHz audio requires 16,000 sequential forward passes.

WaveRNN~\cite{kalchbrenner2018efficient} reduced this cost by using a single gated recurrent unit on quantized samples, combined with subscale prediction and sparsification, achieving real-time synthesis on mobile hardware.

\paragraph{Non-autoregressive vocoders.}
To remove sequential generation entirely, flow-based and generative adversarial network (GAN) architectures were developed. WaveGlow~\cite{prenger2019waveglow} uses normalizing flows --- a sequence of invertible transformations --- to convert Gaussian noise into a waveform in one forward pass, conditioned on a mel-spectrogram and trained by maximizing data log-likelihood.

GAN-based vocoders pushed quality and speed further. MelGAN~\cite{kumar2019melgan} uses a fully convolutional generator conditioned on mel-spectrograms, trained against multi-scale discriminators at different time resolutions. HiFi-GAN~\cite{kong2020hifi} added multi-period discriminators that capture periodic speech patterns, and a generator that processes waveforms at multiple scales simultaneously. HiFi-GAN matches WaveNet quality while running orders of magnitude faster, and has become the standard vocoder in modern TTS systems.

\subsubsection{Two-Step TTS Pipelines}

The dominant two-step TTS architecture combines a neural acoustic model (predicting mel-spectrograms from text) with a neural vocoder (generating waveforms from mel-spectrograms). The two parts are trained separately, which is flexible --- either can be improved or replaced independently --- but means the acoustic model is optimized for spectrogram accuracy rather than final audio quality.

A duration predictor, trained on durations from a pre-trained Tacotron model, decides how many frames each phoneme expands to. The expanded phoneme representations are then processed by a parallel decoder. FastSpeech was about 38 times faster than Tacotron~2 with similar quality.

\subsubsection{End-to-End Models}

Training the acoustic model and vocoder separately creates a mismatch: the vocoder sees natural mel-spectrograms during training but imperfect predicted ones at inference. End-to-end models avoid this by jointly training the full pipeline from text to waveform.

VITS (Variational Inference with adversarial learning for Text-to-Speech)~\cite{kim2021conditional} is the most widely used single-stage model. It combines a variational autoencoder (VAE), a normalizing flow network, and adversarial training in one framework. A posterior encoder maps mel-spectrograms to latent representations; a flow-based prior network learns the distribution of latent variables given text; and a HiFi-GAN decoder generates waveforms from sampled latents. The adversarial and reconstruction losses are trained jointly, so the decoder learns from its own outputs from the start and the prior network is pushed to match the true posterior. At inference, the prior network samples a latent variable from the text alone and the decoder produces the waveform directly, with no intermediate spectrogram. VITS scored higher than Tacotron~2 with WaveGlow in subjective listening tests while generating speech in a single forward pass.
